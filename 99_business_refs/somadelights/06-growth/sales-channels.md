# Sales Channels

## Channel Classification Framework

Every channel is classified into one of four categories:

| Classification | Meaning | Action |
|---------------|---------|--------|
| **CORE** | Essential to the business model. Activate immediately. | Build, optimize, protect |
| **SECONDARY** | Valuable but not essential. Activate when ready. | Plan, pilot, evaluate |
| **EXPERIMENTAL** | Unproven for our model. Test carefully. | Small pilot, measure, decide |
| **AVOID** | Harmful to margin, brand, or operations. Do not use. | Say no, revisit only at scale |

## Channel-by-Channel Analysis

### 1. Direct WhatsApp Ordering

**Classification: CORE — Launch Day**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Customer messages on WhatsApp Business to order, modify, pause, or ask questions. Founder responds personally. |
| **Pros** | Zero setup cost. Every customer already has WhatsApp. Personal relationship built into the channel. No intermediary. Full customer data ownership. |
| **Cons** | Doesn't scale past 100-150 customers without automation. Manual order tracking. No payment integration (separate UPI/Razorpay link). |
| **Margin impact** | Zero commission. 100% margin retained. |
| **Operational complexity** | Low (at < 50 customers). Medium (50-150). High (150+). |
| **Brand alignment** | Perfect. Feels personal, warm, like messaging a trusted friend. |
| **When to use** | Launch day through Month 6+. Always keep as a channel even when website exists. |
| **When it breaks** | At 150+ customers, managing orders via chat becomes unsustainable. That's when the website/ordering system takes over for transactions, but WhatsApp stays for relationship. |

**Recommendation:** This is your primary channel for the first 3 months. Every customer orders, communicates, and provides feedback through WhatsApp. Invest time in setting up WhatsApp Business properly — quick replies, catalog, labels, broadcast lists.

### 2. Simple Website (Landing Page + Ordering)

**Classification: CORE — Week 1**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Single-page website explaining the brand, showing products, linking to WhatsApp for ordering (Phase 1). Add Razorpay subscription checkout (Phase 2, Month 1-2). |
| **Pros** | Credibility signal ("they have a website"). Shareable link for referrals. Can host booklet PDFs, ingredient stories, QR code destinations. SEO benefit over time. |
| **Cons** | Costs Rs 3,000-5,000 to set up (domain + simple builder). Needs occasional updates. |
| **Margin impact** | Negligible cost. Razorpay charges 2% on transactions. |
| **Operational complexity** | Low. Build once, update monthly. |
| **Brand alignment** | Good — clean, educational, trust-building. Must NOT look like an e-commerce store. |
| **When to use** | Week 1 onward. Share the link in every WhatsApp conversation, on every bottle QR code, in every booklet. |

**Phase 1 website (Week 1):**
- One page: hero image, "What is micro-wellness?", 4-6 product cards with photos, "How it works" (subscribe → daily delivery → habit forms), pricing, WhatsApp CTA button
- Host on Carrd.co (Rs 1,500/year) or a simple WordPress site
- Total cost: Rs 3,000-5,000

**Phase 2 website (Month 1-2):**
- Add Razorpay subscription checkout (customer enters plan, pays, subscription auto-created)
- Add customer testimonials section
- Add ingredient story pages (QR codes on bottles link here)

**Recommendation:** Build the simplest possible website in Week 1. It exists to give credibility and host content, not to be a shopping experience. WhatsApp remains the primary ordering channel.

### 3. Subscription-First Ordering System

**Classification: CORE — Month 1**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Default to subscription plans (daily, alternate-day, 3x/week). One-time orders are possible but not promoted. Razorpay Subscriptions handles recurring billing. |
| **Pros** | Predictable revenue. Predictable production. Lower per-order friction (customer decides once, not daily). Higher LTV. |
| **Cons** | Subscription fatigue — some customers resist committing. Need pause/modify flexibility. Payment failures on auto-debit. |
| **Margin impact** | Positive — subscription customers have 2-3x higher LTV than one-time buyers. Razorpay takes 2% per transaction. |
| **Operational complexity** | Medium. Need to track active subscriptions, handle pauses, manage payment failures. |
| **Brand alignment** | Perfect. The entire brand is built on daily habits. Subscription IS the product. |

**Subscription plans:**

| Plan | Frequency | Price | Best For |
|------|-----------|-------|----------|
| Daily Ritual | Every day | Rs 4,500/month (Rs 150/day) | Committed wellness seekers |
| Weekday Wellness | Mon-Fri (22 days) | Rs 3,300/month (Rs 150/day) | Office-goers, weekends at home |
| Alternate Day | 15 days/month | Rs 2,250/month (Rs 150/day) | Budget-conscious, trying it out |
| 3x Week | 12 days/month | Rs 1,800/month (Rs 150/day) | Minimum effective dose |
| Trial | 3 days (one-time) | Rs 350-450 | First-time customers |

**Recommendation:** Frame everything as a subscription. "Which plan works best for your routine?" — not "Do you want to subscribe?" The trial pack is the only one-time offering.

### 4. Own Mobile App

**Classification: AVOID — Until 500+ Customers**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Custom mobile app for ordering, subscription management, wallet, streak tracking, content. |
| **Pros** | Push notifications, richer experience, wallet/streak gamification, professional impression. |
| **Cons** | Costs Rs 2-5 lakhs to build. 3-6 months development. Ongoing maintenance. App store friction (download, sign up). Nobody downloads apps from unknown brands. |
| **Margin impact** | Massive upfront cost with zero revenue impact until significant adoption. |
| **Operational complexity** | Very high. Bug fixes, OS updates, feature requests, crash monitoring. |
| **Brand alignment** | Neutral. Doesn't hurt brand but doesn't help until there's enough content and features to justify an app. |

**Why to avoid now:**
- At 50 customers, you don't need an app. WhatsApp + website handles everything.
- At 200 customers, you still don't need an app. A well-organized Google Sheet + Razorpay + WhatsApp works fine.
- At 500 customers, MAYBE. If your retention data shows that gamification (streaks, wallet, badges) would measurably improve retention, and WhatsApp is becoming unmanageable, then consider a simple app.
- Even at 500, consider a Progressive Web App (PWA) before a native app — cheaper, no app store needed, works on all devices.

**Recommendation:** Do not build an app. Do not hire someone to build an app. Do not even plan an app. When the time comes (500+ subscribers, proven model), you'll know. Until then, it's a distraction that burns cash and attention.

### 5. Swiggy/Zomato

**Classification: SECONDARY — Discovery Only, Month 6+**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | List products on Swiggy/Zomato. Customers discover and order through the platform. Platform handles payment. Delivery by their rider or self-delivery. |
| **Pros** | Massive discovery reach. Built-in trust (customers trust the platform). No marketing needed — the platform brings traffic. |
| **Cons** | 25-35% commission on every order. You lose the customer relationship (they're Swiggy's customer, not yours). No subscription model possible. Listing alongside cheap juice bars destroys premium positioning. Ratings/reviews risk. Delivery time pressure. |
| **Margin impact** | Devastating. At Rs 150 selling price and 30% commission, you keep Rs 105. COGS is Rs 55. That leaves Rs 50 gross margin — barely viable. |
| **Operational complexity** | Medium-High. Platform demands fast preparation, specific packaging, menu management, review responses. |
| **Brand alignment** | Poor. Soma Delights is a wellness habit brand. Swiggy/Zomato is for impulse food orders. Wrong context. |

**If you list (Month 6+):**
- Use it ONLY for discovery. Price 15-20% higher on platforms to offset commission.
- Include a card in every platform order: "Order directly on WhatsApp for better prices and daily delivery. [WhatsApp number]"
- Do NOT list your subscription plans on platforms — subscriptions are direct-only.
- Limit to 2-3 SKUs on the platform (not your full menu).
- Monitor reviews obsessively — one bad review on Swiggy damages more than 10 good WhatsApp conversations.

**Recommendation:** Avoid for the first 6 months. When you list, treat it as a discovery billboard — get them in the door, convert to direct WhatsApp ordering immediately.

### 6. B2B Office Subscriptions

**Classification: SECONDARY — Month 3+**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Approach small-medium IT offices (10-50 employees) in the Kukatpally/Miyapur corridor. Offer daily delivery of 10-30 bottles. Office pays centrally or employees subscribe individually. |
| **Pros** | Volume (10-30 bottles/day per office). Route-efficient (one stop, many bottles). Regular payment from company account. Employee exposure leads to personal subscriptions. |
| **Cons** | Longer sales cycle (2-4 weeks of meetings/proposals). Bulk discount pressure (offices expect 10-15% off). Single point of failure (office cancels = 20 bottles/day gone overnight). Decision-maker churn (HR person leaves, new person has different priorities). |
| **Margin impact** | Mixed. Volume helps COGS efficiency but discount pressure reduces per-unit margin. Rs 130-140/bottle vs. Rs 150 direct. |
| **Operational complexity** | Medium. Need invoicing, consistent large-volume production, corporate communication. |
| **Brand alignment** | Good. "Office wellness program powered by Soma Delights" is a credible positioning. |

**Pilot approach (Month 3):**
1. Start with offices where you already have an individual subscriber (they can introduce you)
2. Offer a 1-week free trial: 10 bottles/day, different products each day
3. Pricing: Rs 135/bottle for 10+/day, Rs 130/bottle for 20+/day
4. Keep it simple: daily delivery, monthly invoice, UPI/bank transfer payment
5. Include ingredient cards with office deliveries — employees become individual subscribers at home

**Recommendation:** Nice-to-have, not need-to-have. Start with 1-2 offices where you have a personal connection. Don't invest in B2B sales outreach until individual subscriber base is stable at 100+.

### 7. Gyms, Cafes, and Wellness Partners

**Classification: SECONDARY — Month 2+**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Supply product to gyms (for trainers and members), health cafes (for resale), yoga studios, wellness centers. |
| **Pros** | Captive audience of health-conscious people. Trainer/staff endorsement is powerful. Physical presence in relevant locations. |
| **Cons** | Margin pressure if selling wholesale (40-50% discount for resale). Quality control (how do they store it?). Brand experience is out of your hands. |
| **Margin impact** | For gyms (free to trainers): cost of Rs 45-55/bottle/day = Rs 1,350-1,650/month per gym. No revenue. But acquisition value of 5-10 subscribers/month = Rs 22,500-45,000/month revenue. Strong ROI. For cafes (wholesale): Rs 75-90/bottle to them, they sell at Rs 150-180. Margin cut in half. |
| **Operational complexity** | Low-Medium. Add to delivery route. |
| **Brand alignment** | Gyms: Excellent. Cafes: Good if the right kind of cafe (health-focused, not generic). Wellness centers: Excellent. |

**Recommended approach:**
- Gyms: Free supply for trainers (2-3 bottles/day). Small display. QR code poster. No wholesale to members — always direct subscription.
- Cafes: ONLY if they are a wellness-focused cafe (like a smoothie bar, organic cafe). Not a regular restaurant. Wholesale at Rs 80-90/bottle, minimum 10/day. But this is not a priority.
- Yoga/wellness studios: Same model as gyms. Free for instructors, display for clients.

### 8. Apartment Pop-Up Kiosks

**Classification: CORE — Launch Channel**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Set up a small sampling table at apartment complex gates on weekends. Free samples, booklets, trial sign-ups. See GTM doc for detailed playbook. |
| **Pros** | Direct face-to-face interaction. Immediate product trial. Captures exactly your target customer (apartment-dwelling professionals). Low cost (Rs 500-1,000/event). Word of mouth within the apartment complex. |
| **Cons** | Time-intensive (3 hours per event). Weather-dependent. RWA permission can be slow. Limited reach (one complex at a time). |
| **Margin impact** | Very positive. Rs 500-1,000 cost per event → 5-8 trials → 3-4 subscriptions = Rs 125-333 CAC. |
| **Operational complexity** | Low. Prepare samples, print materials, show up. |
| **Brand alignment** | Excellent. Face-to-face, community-embedded, educational, zero-pressure. |

**Pop-up calendar target:**

| Month | Frequency | Complexes |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Month 1 | 3-4 events | 2-3 complexes (test which convert best) |
| Month 2 | 6-8 events | 4-5 complexes (double down on winners) |
| Month 3 | 8-12 events | 6-8 complexes (expand to new areas) |
| Month 4+ | 4-6 events | Maintain presence at top converters, explore new corridors |

**Recommendation:** Your single highest-converting acquisition channel after personal referrals. Protect your weekend mornings for pop-ups in Month 1-3.

### 9. Corporate Wellness Pilots

**Classification: EXPERIMENTAL — Month 6+**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | Partner with companies (100+ employees) to offer Soma Delights as part of their wellness program. Company subsidizes or fully funds employee subscriptions. |
| **Pros** | Large volume. Company pays (guaranteed revenue). Premium positioning ("our company cares about employee wellness"). Employee base for individual conversion. |
| **Cons** | Very long sales cycle (3-6 months). Requires proposals, presentations, procurement. Budget cycles may not align. One cancellation = massive revenue hit. Customization demands. |
| **Margin impact** | Depends on negotiation. Corporate programs typically expect 20-30% discount. But guaranteed volume for 6-12 months. |
| **Operational complexity** | High. Corporate communication, SLAs, invoicing, possibly custom packaging/branding. |
| **Brand alignment** | Mixed. Good positioning but risks becoming a "vendor" instead of a "brand." |

**Recommendation:** Do not pursue until Month 6+ and 200+ individual subscribers. By then you have operational maturity, testimonials, and the credibility to approach corporate HR. Start with tech companies in HITEC City that have wellness budgets.

### 10. Marketplaces (Amazon, BigBasket, Flipkart)

**Classification: AVOID**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| **How it works** | List products on Amazon/BigBasket/Flipkart. They handle discovery, payment, delivery. |
| **Pros** | Massive reach. Built-in logistics. |
| **Cons** | 30-40% commission. Cold-pressed drinks need cold chain (expensive on marketplace logistics). You compete on price with mass-market juice brands. No subscription model. No customer relationship. No education touchpoint. Returns/refund policies destroy margin. Impossible to include booklets, ingredient cards, or personal messaging. |
| **Margin impact** | Destructive. Rs 150 selling price - 35% commission - Rs 55 COGS - Rs 20 logistics = Rs -2.50 loss per bottle. You literally lose money on every sale. |
| **Operational complexity** | High. Listings, inventory management, packaging requirements, returns handling. |
| **Brand alignment** | Terrible. Soma Delights listed between "Real Juice" and "Paper Boat" on Amazon destroys every brand signal we've built. |

**Recommendation:** Hard no. Not now, not at 500 customers, not at 5,000. The marketplace model is fundamentally incompatible with a habit-building, relationship-driven, education-first brand. If customers can't differentiate you from packaged juice on a marketplace search results page, you've already lost.

**Only exception:** If you eventually develop shelf-stable products (dehydrated powders, sealed shots with 6-month shelf life), marketplaces might work for those specific SKUs. But not for fresh cold-pressed drinks. Ever.

## Channel Strategy Summary

### Month 0-3: Three Channels Only

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                   │
│   WhatsApp (ordering + CRM + community)           │
│          ↕                                        │
│   Simple Website (credibility + content)           │
│          ↕                                        │
│   Apartment Pop-Ups (acquisition + sampling)       │
│                                                   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**That's it.** No gyms, no B2B, no platforms, no app. Master these three channels before adding complexity.

### Month 3-6: Add Secondary

```
Core:       WhatsApp + Website + Pop-Ups + Subscriptions
Secondary:  Gym Partnerships (2-3) + B2B Pilot (1-2 offices)
```

### Month 6-12: Selective Expansion

```
Core:       WhatsApp + Website + Pop-Ups + Subscriptions
Secondary:  Gyms + B2B Offices + Swiggy/Zomato (discovery)
Pilot:      Corporate Wellness (1-2 companies)
```

## Channel Economics Comparison

| Channel | Revenue per Customer | Commission/Cost | Net Margin | CAC | LTV:CAC |
|---------|---------------------|-----------------|------------|-----|---------|
| Direct WhatsApp | Rs 4,500/month | 0% | 55-60% | Rs 0-100 | 25:1+ |
| Website + Razorpay | Rs 4,500/month | 2% | 53-58% | Rs 0-100 | 25:1+ |
| Pop-up → subscription | Rs 4,500/month | Rs 200 (event cost amortized) | 55-60% | Rs 200-300 | 15:1 |
| Gym referral | Rs 4,500/month | Rs 150 (free product to trainer) | 55-60% | Rs 150-300 | 15:1 |
| B2B office | Rs 3,900-4,200/month | 10-15% discount | 45-50% | Rs 500 (sales time) | 8:1 |
| Swiggy/Zomato | Rs 150/order (one-time) | 25-35% | 15-25% | Rs 0 (platform cost) | 1:1 |
| Amazon/BigBasket | Rs 150/order (one-time) | 30-40% | -2% to 10% | Rs 0 | < 1:1 |

**The math is clear:** Direct channels (WhatsApp, website, pop-ups) deliver 5-15x better economics than platform channels. Every customer you convert from a platform to direct ordering is worth Rs 2,000-3,000 more per year.

---

## Decision Rules

**When someone suggests a new channel, ask these 5 questions:**

1. **Do we own the customer relationship?** If no, be very cautious.
2. **Does the channel support subscription?** If no, it's discovery-only at best.
3. **What's the margin after channel costs?** If below 40%, probably not worth it.
4. **Does the channel align with "education-first, habit-building" positioning?** If no, it dilutes the brand.
5. **Can we execute this without taking attention from our core channels?** If no, defer it.

If a channel fails on 2+ of these questions, classify it as AVOID until circumstances change.

---

*The best channel strategy is the one with the fewest channels executed exceptionally. Add channels only when the current ones are saturated, not when you're bored of them.*
