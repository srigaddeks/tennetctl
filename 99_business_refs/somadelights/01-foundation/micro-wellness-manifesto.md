## I. The Analogy That Explains Everything

In Germany, there is a culture of fresh beer.

Not bottled. Not preserved. Not shipped across the country in refrigerated trucks to sit on a supermarket shelf for weeks. Fresh from the tap. Brewed locally. Consumed the same day.

The beer has a short shelf life. That shelf life is not a flaw. It is proof of life. Proof that nothing was added to make it survive a supply chain it was never meant to enter.

In Hyderabad, you can walk into a micro-brewery and drink a beer that was brewed that week, in the same building, by people you can talk to. You can see the tanks. You can ask questions. You can taste the difference.

Craft breweries do not make beer for shelf life. They make it for freshness, for local consumption, for community. A Dwaraka Das Colony regular at a neighborhood micro-brewery is not a barcode scan. He is a known face. The brewer knows his preferred style. There is a relationship. There is accountability. There is quality that comes from caring about what you hand to someone you know.

We asked one question:

**What if we applied the same philosophy to wellness?**

Not supplements in plastic bottles with two-year shelf lives, manufactured in a factory in Pune that you will never visit, by a corporation that spends more on celebrity endorsements than on ingredients.

Not pasteurized juice that sat in a Nashik warehouse for three weeks, stripped of its nutrition by the same heat treatment meant to "preserve" it. Preserved, yes. Alive, no.

Not branded wellness drinks loaded with stabilizers, emulsifiers, and synthetic vitamins so they can survive a supply chain designed for Coca-Cola — shipped from Maharashtra to a Big Bazaar in Kukatpally, sitting on a shelf for months, waiting for someone to mistake the word "natural" on the label for something that means what it used to mean.

We wanted something different.

**Fresh. Local. Alive.**

Made this morning at 4am. Packed by 5:30am. Delivered by 7am. Consumed today.

No preservatives, because preservatives exist to serve supply chains, not people. No pasteurization, because pasteurization kills the nutrition along with the bacteria. No HPP processing, because extending shelf life is extending the distance between the customer and the truth.

Short shelf life. Small batches. Small delivery radius. Real relationships with every single customer.

**That is Soma Delights. The micro-brewery of wellness.**

## II. "If It Has Preservatives, It Is Dead. We Serve It Alive."

This is not a tagline. It is the operating constraint that governs every production decision Soma makes.

### What This Means in Practice

A preservative exists to stop biological processes. Biological processes — oxidation, fermentation, microbial activity — are what happen to real food made from real ingredients. A preservative arrests those processes. By definition, that is also what death is at the cellular level: the cessation of biological processes.

A fresh cold-pressed juice is alive in the only sense that matters to nutrition: it is still changing. Enzymes are active. Vitamins are degrading slowly and naturally. Flavor is evolving. It is real, and reality is temporary.

When Soma presses a green juice at 4am, that bottle contains:

- Active enzymes from the spinach, apple, ginger, and lemon that your digestive system uses directly
- Vitamin C that begins degrading on contact with air and light — which is why we seal immediately and deliver fast
- Chlorophyll that has not yet been broken down by heat or time
- Live phytonutrients that pasteurization or HPP would reduce or eliminate

That bottle expires in 24–48 hours. Not because we failed to preserve it. Because we chose not to. Choosing not to preserve it is what keeps it alive.

A Rs 89 packaged orange juice in a tetra pack at a convenience store lasts six months on the shelf. Ask yourself: what was done to that juice to make that possible? The answer is pasteurization (heat-treated to kill everything alive in it), possibly HPP (high-pressure processed to further extend shelf life), added synthetic Vitamin C to compensate for the real Vitamin C destroyed in processing, and preservatives to prevent spoilage during the months between factory and your hand.

That juice is technically safe. It is not alive.

### The "Alive vs. Dead" Food Spectrum

Where does urban Indian eating currently sit?

```
ALIVE                                                          DEAD
  |                                                              |
  |  Fresh cold-pressed    Pasteurized packaged    Soda /
  |  juice (Soma) ──────── juice (Tropicana) ───── energy drink ── Fast food
  |  (24-48 hr shelf life) (3-6 month shelf life)  (12+ months)    (ultra-processed)
  |
  |  Most urban Indian households spend most of their food
  |  budget in the right half of this spectrum.
```

This is not a moral judgment. It is a fact about how urban Indian food infrastructure works. Fast food chains, delivery platforms, and packaged beverage brands have optimized for the right side of that spectrum because shelf life is the only way to run a national supply chain. The infrastructure rewards dead food.

Soma's answer is not to make a dead product that markets itself as alive. It is to build a model — micro-scale, local, direct-delivery — where alive products are operationally viable.

## III. The Rs 2,000 Fast Food Swap

Most urban Indian families in the Kukatpally–Miyapur corridor spend between Rs 1,500 and Rs 3,000 per month on fast food and food delivery. McDonald's, KFC, Domino's, Swiggy, Zomato. Rs 350 for a meal that arrives in a plastic container, is eaten in 10 minutes, and is forgotten by the next morning.

This is not an indictment of those choices. It is context for a comparison.

What does Rs 2,000 of that monthly fast food spend buy at Soma Delights?

### The Fast Food Rs 2,000

- 5–6 Swiggy orders from a mid-range restaurant
- What your body receives: refined carbohydrates, seed oils heated beyond their smoke point, refined sugar, sodium, preservatives, flavor enhancers, plastic microparticles from packaging, and zero functional nutrition
- What it costs the environment: plastic packaging, delivery fuel, platform fees (Swiggy/Zomato take 25–30% from the restaurant, which means quality ingredients are the first thing restaurants cut)
- Where the money goes: Rs 400–600 to Swiggy/Zomato as platform commission, Rs 200–300 to delivery infrastructure, the rest split between the restaurant's food cost, rent, and labor

### The Soma Rs 2,000

- A 30-day morning juice subscription (one 300ml cold-pressed juice daily)
- What your body receives every morning: live enzymes, bioavailable vitamins and minerals, functional ingredients (turmeric, ginger, amla, ashwagandha, moringa depending on your blend), no preservatives, no seed oils, no refined sugar, no synthetic additives
- What it costs the environment: returnable glass bottles (we collect and sterilize), no platform middlemen, locally sourced produce from Ranga Reddy district and Hyderabad wholesale markets
- Where the money goes: 60–65% to ingredients and production, 20–25% to delivery and operations, 10–15% to overhead. Zero to Instagram ads. Zero to food delivery platforms.

### The Contrast

| | Fast Food Rs 2,000 | Soma Rs 2,000 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| What you get | 5-6 meals | 30 daily juices |
| Nutritional value | Near zero (macros + empty calories) | High (micronutrients, enzymes, adaptogens) |
| Where money goes | 30% to platforms | 0% to platforms |
| Shelf life of product | Made fresh, but in degraded oils and processed ingredients | Pressed fresh that morning |
| Relationship with maker | None — anonymous transaction | Personal — you talk to us on WhatsApp |
| 90-day impact on your body | Cumulative inflammation, gut stress, energy crashes | Better digestion, clearer skin, steadier energy |
| Habit formed | Reactive hunger — order when hungry | Proactive daily ritual — drink before the day begins |

This is not about guilt. Nobody should feel guilty about a Saturday biryani from Swiggy. The question is: do you want Rs 2,000 of your monthly food spend to work for your body, or against it? That choice exists. Most people do not realize it does because nobody has offered them a daily fresh alternative at this price point in this city.

Soma does.

## IV. Habit Over Hype: What We Actually Sell

We do not sell detox.

We do not sell weight loss promises, before-and-after photos, 7-day cleanses, "reset" programs, or miracle cure testimonials. We do not make pharmaceutical claims, use pseudo-scientific language, or imply that a bottle of juice will reverse a decade of nutritional neglect.

We sell one thing: a daily 7am habit that compounds over 90 days.

### What Happens in 90 Days

A single glass of cold-pressed juice does not change your health. Drinking one every morning at 7am, before anything else enters your body, for 90 consecutive days does something different.

It is not a dramatic transformation. It is a slow, quiet accumulation of small signals sent to your body every single morning. You are giving your digestive system something clean and live to start the day. You are front-loading micronutrients before coffee, before breakfast, before the chaos of the day begins. You are building a ritual — and rituals, by their nature, compound.

What customers report after 90 days is not dramatic. It is:

- Lighter digestion — less bloating, more regular
- Better skin — reduced inflammation shows in the face first
- Steadier energy — fewer afternoon crashes
- Improved sleep — the body running cleaner tends to sleep more efficiently
- Clearer thinking — subtle, but reported consistently by people who are paying attention

None of these results are from any single ingredient. They are from consistency. The turmeric that does nothing on Day 1 is doing something on Day 60 because it has been there every morning. The ginger that felt sharp on Day 3 feels natural by Day 30 because your gut has adapted and the inflammation it was fighting has reduced.

We track this. We ask customers at Day 30, Day 60, and Day 90 what they are noticing. We publish those observations — anonymized and unedited — on our website. Not testimonials. Observations. There is a difference.

**We are not in the juice business. We are in the habit-building business.**

## V. Education as Brand: We Open-Source Our Knowledge

No brand in India's packaged beverage space openly teaches its customers what is in their product, why each ingredient was chosen, and how the body actually uses it.

They hide behind marketing.

They use words like "natural," "fresh," "pure," "organic," "goodness" because these words imply transparency without requiring it. They do not publish recipes. They do not explain the sourcing chain. They do not tell you why their product costs what it costs. They do not tell you what happens to nutrients in pasteurization. They do not want you to know — because if you knew, you might realize the price-to-value ratio does not hold.

Soma does the opposite.

### What We Publish

**Recipes:** Every Soma blend has a public recipe. If you want to make Soma Green at home — 150g spinach, 1 medium apple, 15g ginger, half a lemon, 250ml water, cold-pressed — you have everything you need. We will tell you the exact quantities. The recipe is ours. We have no interest in protecting it.

**Ingredient sourcing:** We publish where our produce comes from. Tomatoes from Bowenpally market, amla from Ranga Reddy district farms, ginger from Andhra suppliers who grow it without synthetic pesticide use. Not because it is legally required. Because it is the right thing to do.

**Cost breakdowns:** What Rs 129 for a Soma Green actually pays for: Rs 35–40 on raw ingredients (day-of procurement, no advance batch buying), Rs 15–20 on production (labor, equipment amortization, glass bottle cost), Rs 20–25 on delivery, Rs 20–25 on overhead and operating costs, Rs 15–20 margin. This is not a high-margin business. It is a fair-margin business built on volume and loyalty.

**What we do not know:** When we have not verified a sourcing claim, we say so. When we are still testing a recipe, we say so. When a customer asks a question we cannot answer confidently, we research it and come back.

An educated customer who understands why their juice is worth Rs 129 is a better customer than an ignorant one who might switch to a Rs 89 packaged alternative the moment they forget why they chose fresh. Education is not charity. It is retention strategy built on truth.

## VI. The Micro-Wellness Promise

We are not pharmaceutical wellness. We are not selling diagnoses, treatments, or clinical outcomes. We make no claims that fall under drug, supplement, or medical device regulation.

Micro-wellness is the 1% daily improvements that accumulate into visible, felt differences over 90 days.

### What "Micro" Means Here

**Micro = Small, Daily, Consistent**
Not a 30-day detox. Not a dramatic intervention. One glass, every morning, forever. The power is in the unbroken chain, not the intensity of any single day.

**Micro = Local and Human-Scale**
The micro-brewery does not compete with Budweiser. It serves its neighborhood. Soma serves its apartment complexes and gated communities within a 5km radius. This is not a limitation. It is the architecture of freshness. Every kilometer added to the supply chain is a compromise on quality. We refuse to compromise, so we refuse to scale beyond what freshness allows.

**Micro = Visible in 90 Days**
We ask customers to commit to 90 days before forming an opinion. The cumulative effects of daily fresh nutrition take that long to register — in digestion, in skin, in energy, in sleep. We do not promise before-and-after photos. We promise a 90-day arc of small improvements that compound into something you can feel.

### The Four Pillars of Micro-Wellness

**Sleep better.** The body's recovery and inflammation cycles run overnight. When you front-load live nutrition in the morning, you reduce the daily oxidative stress load. Less oxidative stress across 60 days means better sleep quality. This is not a claim. It is a mechanism.

**Digest better.** Live enzymes in cold-pressed juice begin working in your gut within 20 minutes of consumption. Daily enzyme support over months rebuilds gut microbiome balance and reduces the bloating and irregularity that define most urban Indian gut health. Eat less fried food, drink one live juice daily, and your gut will notice within 30 days.

**Feel lighter.** Not lighter as in weight. Lighter as in the absence of the heaviness that processed food creates — the sluggishness after a heavy meal, the fogginess that follows a late-night delivery order, the inflammation that presents as joint discomfort or skin issues. Remove the daily processed-food load and replace it with one fresh juice ritual. The lightness is the absence of what was there before.

**Think clearer.** Gut-brain axis research is still developing, but the signal is consistent: a well-functioning gut reduces the cognitive fog associated with chronic low-grade inflammation. Customers who have been on Soma for 60+ days report this more than any other effect. It is the last one to arrive and the most surprising.

## VII. Why Hyderabad, Why Now

Hyderabad in 2025 is at a specific inflection point that makes micro-wellness viable here before it would be viable in most other Indian cities.

**IT culture has created a health-aware professional class.** Lakhs of tech workers in Hyderabad's IT corridor are exposed to global wellness content, have disposable income, live in apartments with refrigerators, and have already adopted some version of a morning routine. They are aware of cold-pressed juice. They have tried imported wellness products. They are ready for a local version that actually delivers on the freshness promise.

**Dense apartment living creates delivery density.** A single apartment complex in Miyapur with 400 units and a 15% Soma penetration is 60 customers within one delivery stop. That density makes the unit economics of fresh daily delivery viable in a way it is not in lower-density neighborhoods or smaller cities.

**Rising health awareness post-2020.** The pandemic reshaped how urban Indians think about immunity, gut health, and daily habits. This is not trend. It is a permanent shift in priority. Families who never thought about what their children ate for breakfast are now asking questions about nutrition. That awareness is the market.

**No credible local competitor.** The cold-pressed juice market in Hyderabad exists but is fragmented. A few gyms and cafes sell cold-pressed, but without daily delivery infrastructure, subscription models, or the educational layer that creates retention. The market is open for a brand that takes it seriously.

**Hyderabad's Telugu-Hindu-Hindi culture supports wellness as a concept.** This city already has deep roots in Ayurvedic and naturopathic wellness traditions. Families that give their children haldi doodh, that fast on Ekadashi for digestive reset, that prize amla and neem and turmeric — these families are not unfamiliar with the idea of functional daily nutrition. Soma speaks their existing language, in three scripts, with modern delivery infrastructure behind it.

## VIII. What We Will Never Be

These are not aspirational constraints. They are structural ones. Violating them would require changing what Soma is at its core.

**We will never be a brand that pays Instagram Rs 15 for every Rs 100 in revenue.**
The math of Instagram advertising — cost per click, conversion rates, customer acquisition cost — does not work for a subscription business with Rs 129 average order values unless you are building a brand that eventually exits to a conglomerate. That is not what we are building. Our marketing budget goes to the Partner Program — direct cash and credits to the customers who refer their neighbors. Every rupee that would have gone to Meta goes back to the community.

**We will never add preservatives to extend shelf life.**
Not if demand exceeds production capacity. Not if cold chain fails in summer. Not ever. The moment we add a preservative, we are no longer alive. We would rather turn away orders than compromise the product's fundamental nature. Demand constraint is a capacity problem we will solve with another micro-kitchen, not a chemistry problem we will solve with sodium benzoate.

**We will never hide our recipes.**
If you can make it at home, you should. Soma exists for the mornings you cannot find 45 minutes to wash, chop, press, and clean a juicer before 7am. We are convenience with integrity, not a proprietary formula protecting market share.

**We will never call ourselves organic without proof.**
"Organic" in the Indian market is a marketing word used without verification by 80% of the brands that deploy it. Soma uses produce that we source from local markets and farms that we have visited. Where we have verified that a supplier uses minimal or no synthetic pesticide use, we say so. Where we have not verified, we say nothing. We will pursue formal organic certification as supply chain allows — but we will not claim it before we can prove it.

**We will never become a platform brand.**
Soma will not list on Swiggy Instamart, Blinkit, or Zepto. Those platforms take 25–30% commission, require 10–30 minute delivery windows that are incompatible with cold chain, and turn Soma into a commodity next to a dozen competitors with no context or relationship. We deliver direct. Our customer knows us. We know our customer. That relationship is worth more than the reach a platform provides, and it is what makes the product work.

**We will never serve anonymous customers at scale in exchange for freshness.**
Growth by opening more micro-kitchens preserves freshness. Growth by centralizing production and extending delivery radius destroys it. We will never trade the former for the latter regardless of how much it would simplify the business. A Soma customer 15km from the micro-kitchen is not a customer we can serve well. We will not pretend otherwise.

## IX. The Closing Statement

Every brand has a story. Most of them are manufactured by agencies that specialize in making ordinary products sound extraordinary.

This manifesto is not a story. It is a set of operating constraints — each one a boundary we have chosen to build inside. Every statement limits what Soma can do. That is the point. The constraints are not sacrifices. They are the architecture of a brand that is genuinely different, not just differently marketed.

The micro-brewery does not compete with Kingfisher. It does not want to. It serves a different customer, at a different scale, with a different relationship, and a fundamentally different product. Its short shelf life is not a manufacturing limitation. It is proof of what it is.

Soma does not compete with Tropicana. It does not want to. It serves a neighborhood, at human scale, with real relationships, and a product that was alive four hours ago.

We are the micro-brewery of wellness.

Every bottle we make expires in 48 hours.

That is not a problem.

**That is the point.**
