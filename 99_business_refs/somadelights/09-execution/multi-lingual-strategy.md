**Status:** Operational Reference

## 1. Why Multi-Lingual Is Non-Negotiable

Soma Delights launches in the Kukatpally–Miyapur–Chandanagar corridor. This is not a homogeneous market. A single apartment complex on JNTU Road can contain Telugu-speaking families who have lived in Hyderabad for three generations, Hindi-speaking migrants from UP, Bihar, and Rajasthan who moved here for IT or manufacturing work, and English-speaking tech professionals who read their WhatsApp in English and expect brand communication to match their register.

If you launch English-only, you reach roughly 30–40% of your addressable households in this corridor. You also signal — loudly and unintentionally — that Soma Delights is a brand for a certain kind of person. That is the opposite of what we are building.

The micro-wellness movement we are building is community-first. It lives in apartment WhatsApp groups, in conversations between neighbors, in the moment a mother tells her daughter-in-law about the turmeric shot she has been taking every morning. That conversation happens in Telugu. The referral from an IT worker to his flatmate from Lucknow happens in Hindi. Neither of those conversations happens if our content cannot meet people where they are.

**Multi-lingual is not a nice-to-have. It is table stakes for this market.**

### The Corridor Demographics

The Kukatpally–Miyapur–Chandanagar belt is one of Hyderabad's densest residential corridors. Conservative estimates for the target customer base (apartment-dwelling, health-aware, Rs 30,000+ household income):

| Language Group | Estimated Share | Profile |
|---|---|---|
| Telugu-speaking | 55–60% | Telangana-origin families, multi-generational Hyderabadis, local professionals |
| Hindi-speaking | 25–30% | North Indian migrants in IT, retail, manufacturing; students; PG residents |
| English-primary | 15–20% | Tech professionals, returning NRIs, English-medium educated households who code-switch fluidly |

These groups overlap. A Telugu engineer reads English at work and converses in Hindi with his North Indian flatmates. But the language that reaches him emotionally — the one that makes him forward a WhatsApp message to his parents — is Telugu. That is the insight.

## 2. Language Priority: Phased, Not Simultaneous

### Tier 1 — Launch, Day 1

**English (Default/Brand Language)**
All content originates in English. This is the brand's source of truth. Internal documents, legal filings, FSSAI compliance, investor materials, and all operational SOPs stay English-only.

Customer-facing register: clean, warm, modern. Confident without arrogance. Never startup-jargon. "Cold-pressed. No preservatives. Delivered by 7am." That is the English voice.

**Telugu (Primary Vernacular)**
Hyderabad is a Telugu-speaking city at its core. Telugu content gets full translation treatment — not summarized, not abbreviated. Same depth as English.

Register: warm, family-oriented, grounded. The tone a trusted neighbor uses when recommending something. Telugu wellness messaging resonates around family health, seasonal wellbeing, digestive care, and the practical ("what does it actually do for my body?"). Lead with benefit, not aspiration.

Audience: Telugu-speaking households across Kukatpally, Miyapur, Chandanagar, KPHB Colony, Nizampet, Bachupally.

**Hindi (Secondary Vernacular — Day 1, not Month 2)**

The original plan was to phase Hindi to Month 2. Updated recommendation: **Hindi launches Day 1 alongside Telugu.** Reason: the Miyapur–Bachupally corridor has a substantial Hindi-speaking migrant population living in PGs and shared apartments. These customers often lack access to home-cooked wellness remedies — kadha, haldi doodh, amla juice — because they live without full kitchen access. Soma Delights fills that exact gap. Starting Hindi on Day 1 captures this segment from the first week rather than asking them to wait.

Register: health-aspirational, energetic, self-investment framing. "Apna khayal rakh." Wellness as something you do for yourself when you are working hard and far from home.

### Tier 2 — Phase 2 (Conditional)

**Kannada**
Add when any of the following conditions are met:
- Active customer base exceeds 200 households, OR
- Delivery routes extend into Gachibowli, Kondapur, Nanakramguda (Karnataka-origin tech workforce belt), OR
- Bangalore corridor expansion begins

Do not add Kannada on a calendar trigger. Add it on a demand trigger. Keep a Kannada-fluent contact identified from early customers so the transition is fast when the trigger is reached.

## 4. Translation Workflow (Bootstrap Model)

Soma cannot afford a translation agency at launch and should not need one. The best translators for Soma's voice are bilingual customers who live in this corridor, understand the wellness context, and communicate the way our customers communicate.

### Identifying Community Translators

From the first 50 customers, identify:

**Telugu translators (2 people):**
- Person 1: a woman in her 30s–40s with family-health context who understands how wellness conversations happen at home. Her instinct for tone will be invaluable for the booklet and ingredient cards.
- Person 2: a younger person (25–32) who is active on Instagram in Telugu and understands how social captions should sound. Different register from Person 1 — both needed.

**Hindi translators (2 people):**
- Person 1: someone who grew up in a Hindi-speaking household and lives in Hyderabad — they naturally code-switch the way Soma's Hindi customers do. Hindustani register, not Shudh Hindi.
- Person 2: cross-check reviewer. Any bilingual Hindi-Telugu or Hindi-English speaker who can spot tone drift.

**Compensation:**
- Partner Program credits: Rs 300–500/month equivalent in free product for active contribution
- Named acknowledgment in the wellness booklet ("Community Voices" section)
- First access to new products before public launch

These are early believers, not contractors. Treat them accordingly.

### Step-by-Step Workflow

```
Step 1: Founder writes content in English — final, approved version only.
        Never send a draft for translation.

Step 2: Translator receives EN copy WITH context brief:
        - Who is the audience?
        - What is the purpose (inform / motivate / instruct)?
        - What is the register (warm / functional / urgent)?

Step 3: Translator produces TE or HI version.

Step 4: Second bilingual reviewer reads for naturalness.
        One question: "Does this sound like a person, or a pamphlet?"
        If it sounds like a pamphlet, send it back.

Step 5: Founder gives final approval before any publication.

Step 6: Translated content logged in translation ledger:
        (EN original | TE version | HI version | date | translator | reviewer)
```

The translation ledger builds a brand glossary over time and makes future translations faster. Product names, wellness terms, and recurring phrases get standardized once and reused.

### Critical Translation Rules

- **No machine translation for customer-facing content.** Google Translate and AI tools produce Telugu and Hindi that reads like a government circular. Soma's voice is warm and conversational. Every word must feel like a person wrote it — because a person did.
- **Never translate product names.** "Soma Green" stays "Soma Green" in all three languages. Brand names do not change.
- **Use conversational register, not literary register.** Conversational Telugu is "vyavaharika" (వ్యావహారిక), not "grandhika" (గ్రాంధిక). "తాజా జ్యూస్" not "నూతన రసం." The same principle applies in Hindi: Hindustani register, not Shudh Sanskrit-heavy Hindi.
- **English loanwords are fine.** In conversational Telugu and Hindi, "juice," "fresh," "vitamin," "protein," "subscription" are standard. Do not force translations of words people already say in English.
- **Numbers and prices in standard numerals.** Use 1, 2, 3 and Rs notation across all languages. Not Telugu numerals, not Devanagari numerals. Commerce is standardized.

## 5. Tone Differences by Language

These are not translations. They are tone adaptations. The same fact, expressed differently for different emotional registers.

**English:** Brand-forward, clean, slightly minimal. Confidence without arrogance. "Cold-pressed. No preservatives. Delivered by 7am." Direct. Declarative. Modern.

**Telugu:** Warm, relational, community-embedded. The emotional register is a trusted neighbor making a genuine recommendation — not a brand speaking at you. Lead with family benefit, seasonal health, digestive care. "మీ శరీరానికి ఏం కావాలో అది ఇస్తాం" (We give your body what it needs) carries more weight in Telugu than any marketing claim.

**Hindi:** Aspirational-health, energetic, self-investment. The emotional register is about taking care of yourself when you are working hard and far from home. "Apna khayal rakh" — take care of yourself — is the emotional anchor. For migrant workers in shared accommodations, Soma is a small daily ritual that connects them to clean, home-style wellness. Lead with personal vitality and the idea that this is something you deserve.

## 6. WhatsApp Multi-Lingual Protocol

WhatsApp is Soma's primary customer communication channel. Language here matters more than anywhere else — it is a personal, conversational medium where brand voice lives or dies.

### Language Preference Capture

- **At sign-up:** Ask directly: "Which language do you prefer for messages? English / Telugu / Hindi"
- **In CRM:** Tag each customer: `lang:en`, `lang:te`, `lang:hi`
- **Changeable anytime:** Customer can switch by messaging "Telugu please" or "Hindi" at any time

### Broadcast List Structure

Maintain three separate broadcast lists:
- `Soma Updates — English`
- `Soma Updates — Telugu` (సోమా అప్‌డేట్స్)
- `Soma Updates — Hindi` (सोमा अपडेट्स)

**Never send a Telugu message to an English-preference customer.** It signals carelessness. The whole point of capturing language preference is to use it without exception.

### Mixed-Language Community Group

For the main Soma WhatsApp community group (as distinct from broadcast lists), use a mixed-language format with explicit language tags. This reflects how Hyderabad actually communicates, and preserves the network effect of a single community rather than fragmenting into three parallel groups.

Format:
```
[EN] Good morning! Your Monday Soma Green was pressed at 4:30am.
Consume by tomorrow 4:30am. What did you notice this week?

[TE] శుభోదయం! మీ సోమవారం Soma Green ఈ రోజు తెల్లవారు 4:30కి
ప్రెస్ చేశాం. రేపు 4:30 కల్లా తాగండి. ఈ వారం ఏమైనా గమనించారా?

[HI] सुप्रभात! आपका सोमवार का Soma Green आज सुबह 4:30 बजे
प्रेस किया गया। कल 4:30 बजे तक पी लें। इस हफ्ते क्या अनुभव किया?
```

**When to split the community group:** when total membership exceeds 150 and the group becomes noisy enough to reduce engagement. At that point, language-specific groups become viable. Keep the main group active as the Hyderabad-wide community anchor.

### Voice Notes

Telugu and Hindi voice notes for delivery confirmations are one of Soma's highest-leverage community-building tools. A 15-second "Good morning, your juice is on the way" in Telugu, recorded by a real person in a warm natural voice, builds loyalty that no written message can match. Prioritize this from Month 1 for Telugu. Add Hindi voice notes by Month 2.

## 7. App and Website Language Implementation

### Website URL Structure

```
somadelights.com/en/     → English (default fallback)
somadelights.com/te/     → Telugu
somadelights.com/hi/     → Hindi
```

Subpaths, not subdomains — single domain authority for SEO, simpler SSL and hosting, shared analytics.

### Default Language Logic

```
1. Returning visitor → use saved preference (cookie/localStorage)
2. New visitor → check browser language setting
   Browser = Telugu → show Telugu
   Browser = Hindi  → show Hindi
   All others       → show English
3. User can always override via language toggle in header
```

### Language Toggle

- Location: header, right side (near cart or account)
- Label: EN / తె / हि (after Kannada: + ಕ)
- Behavior: switches all content, preserves current page URL
- Persistence: stores preference, remembered on next visit

### SEO for Multi-Lingual

Every page needs `hreflang` tags:

```html
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="en" href="https://somadelights.com/en/menu" />
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="te" href="https://somadelights.com/te/menu" />
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="hi" href="https://somadelights.com/hi/menu" />
<link rel="alternate" hreflang="x-default" href="https://somadelights.com/en/menu" />
```

Telugu and Hindi pages rank for queries in those languages with significantly lower competition than English wellness keywords. This is a discoverability advantage, not just an accessibility feature.

## 8. Label Regulations: FSSAI Compliance

Under FSSAI regulations for packaged foods:
- Product name in English: **mandatory**
- Nutritional information in English: **mandatory**
- Manufacturing date, best before date, net quantity in English: **mandatory**
- Regional language additions: **permitted and recommended**, not mandatory

For Soma Delights:
- All labels must carry English compliance fields (FSSAI license number, ingredient list, net quantity, press/expiry date, registered address)
- Adding Telugu on labels is recommended and planned from Day 1 — it signals local authenticity and is the only regional language needed for Hyderabad compliance purposes
- Hindi on labels: optional at launch given space constraints. Hindi-speaking customers in Hyderabad generally read English. Revisit when label redesign is scheduled or when scale justifies a separate label run

**Label priority order:** English (compliance) → Telugu (primary vernacular) → Hindi (secondary, Phase 2 label redesign)

## 9. Booklet Strategy: Separate Language Editions

The wellness booklet is a core education and retention tool. It must exist in all supported languages, but as separate printed editions — not a single cramped bilingual document.

### Why Separate Editions

- **Design integrity.** Bilingual booklets require smaller fonts or double the pages. In Soma's greyscale premium design system, cramped text destroys the aesthetic.
- **Better reading experience.** A Telugu reader wants to read Telugu continuously, not scan past English paragraphs.
- **Cost difference is marginal.** Printing 100 Telugu booklets costs Rs 8,000–12,000. The design time saved by not making a bilingual layout work cleanly is worth more.

**Exception:** ingredient cards are short enough to be bilingual and benefit from it — ingredient name in two languages on one card adds richness rather than clutter.

### Edition Plan

| Edition | Timeline | Print Run | Estimated Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| English | Launch | 200 copies | Rs 15,000–20,000 |
| Telugu | Week 4–6 | 100 copies | Rs 8,000–12,000 |
| Hindi | Week 8–10 | 100 copies | Rs 8,000–12,000 |
| Kannada | Phase 2 | 50 copies | Rs 5,000–8,000 |

Translation budget per edition: Rs 3,000–5,000 (freelancer + community review).

## 10. Content Calendar: Language Tags

Every item in the weekly content calendar must be tagged with its language requirements. This makes it impossible to accidentally publish without completing translation.

| Tag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `[ALL]` | Needs EN + TE + HI before publication |
| `[EN+TE]` | English and Telugu only |
| `[EN]` | English only (internal or compliance) |
| `[TE-ORIGINAL]` | Telugu-first content, no EN version needed |
| `[HI-ORIGINAL]` | Hindi-first content, no EN version needed |
| `[SOCIAL-TE]` | Telugu social content, EN caption optional |
| `[SOCIAL-HI]` | Hindi social content, EN caption optional |

Mark the translator responsible and the review deadline as part of the content calendar entry. Translation is part of the production pipeline, not an afterthought.

### Weekly Social Cadence

| Day | Language | Content Type |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | English | Product or wellness tip |
| Tuesday | Telugu | Same content, translated + adapted |
| Wednesday | English | Behind-the-scenes or transparency post |
| Thursday | Hindi | Wellness tip in Hindi (adapted for migrant audience) |
| Friday | Telugu or Hindi | Customer story or Partner highlight |
| Saturday | English | Weekend recipe or ingredient feature |
| Sunday | Rest | No posting |

## 11. Quality Control

### The Amma Test

Before publishing any Telugu or Hindi content, apply one test:

> "Would my mother understand this? Would it sound natural if she read it aloud?"

If the translation reads like a government notice, a textbook, or a machine output — reject it and rewrite. Do not publish and fix later. Incorrect tone in Telugu or Hindi is not a minor error; it tells the reader this brand was not actually made for them.

### Back-Translation Check (Critical Copy Only)

For content that carries health or nutritional claims — ingredient cards, the booklet's wellness sections, any claim about what the product does to the body — run a back-translation check:

1. Translate EN → TE (translator A)
2. A different bilingual person translates TE back to EN without seeing the original (translator B)
3. Compare the two English versions
4. If meaning has drifted, revise the Telugu translation

Back-translation catches semantic drift that sounds fine in Telugu but has shifted from the original intended claim. This is a compliance risk and a trust risk.

### Common Translation Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Consequence | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| Using literary Telugu (grandhika) instead of conversational Telugu (vyavaharika) | Sounds like a 1970s government pamphlet | Specify conversational register in translator brief |
| Using Shudh Sanskrit-heavy Hindi instead of Hindustani | Alienates North Indian migrant audience who speaks Hindustani | Specify Hindustani; test with a real Hindi-speaker from that community |
| Forcing English loanwords into local-language equivalents nobody uses | Sounds unnatural, creates confusion | Accept "juice," "vitamin," "protein," "subscription" as they are |
| Translating product names | Destroys brand consistency | Hard rule: product names never translate |
| Telugu/Hindi text exceeding label or UI space | Layout breaks | Test with longest translated string before finalizing design |

## 12. Phase 2 Trigger: Kannada

Add Kannada translation to the production pipeline when any of the following conditions are met:

- Active customer base exceeds 200 households
- Delivery routes extend into Gachibowli, Kondapur, Nanakramguda, or Manikonda (significant Karnataka-origin tech workforce)
- Bangalore corridor expansion has a confirmed launch date within 6 months
- A Kannada-speaking customer volunteer emerges organically and proactively offers to help

**Keep a Kannada contact identified from the existing customer base at all times.** When the trigger is reached, the first translations (WhatsApp messages, ingredient cards, social posts) should be ready within 2 weeks. Prepare for this; do not improvise it.

Priority order at Kannada launch: WhatsApp messages → ingredient cards → social content → website → app UI → booklet.

## 14. Operating Principles Summary

1. English is the origin language. All content starts here and is finalized here before translation begins.
2. Telugu is the primary community language. Every piece of customer-facing content gets a Telugu version, every time, without exception.
3. Hindi launches on Day 1. The Miyapur–Bachupally corridor has a large Hindi-speaking migrant population that should not wait.
4. Translations are done by trusted community members compensated via Partner Program, not agencies.
5. Tone adaptation matters as much as linguistic accuracy. Each language has its own emotional register — adapt to it.
6. Language tags in WhatsApp broadcasts ensure every customer receives content in their preferred language.
7. FSSAI labels require English; Telugu on labels builds local trust from first contact.
8. Kannada is Phase 2, triggered by demand signals not calendar dates. Maintain a contact ready.
9. Quality control is non-negotiable. The Amma Test applies to everything. Back-translation applies to health claims.
10. Multi-lingual is not a cost center. It is a customer acquisition and retention engine for the 60–70% of the corridor who are not English-primary.
