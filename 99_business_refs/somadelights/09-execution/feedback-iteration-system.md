# Feedback & Iteration System

> Feedback is not complaint management. It is product intelligence.

Every piece of customer feedback is data — to improve the product, sharpen operations, and deepen the experience. The goal is not to respond to noise but to extract signal and act on it systematically, without letting it derail the core mission.

---

## 1. Philosophy

Soma is a micro-wellness brand. The feedback loop is the product development process. There is no R&D lab, no focus group, no product team. The customers are the product team.

This means:

- Feedback is collected deliberately, not reactively
- Every complaint is a prototype failure, not a customer management problem
- Positive feedback is evidence of what to protect, not just praise
- The brand philosophy — short shelf life, no preservatives, no sweeteners — is not up for negotiation based on feedback. If a customer pushes against these, educate, do not compromise.

---

## 2. Feedback Channels

Channels in priority order:

### Channel 1 — WhatsApp (primary)

Customers naturally message on WhatsApp. This is the primary feedback surface. The goal is not to suppress it but to capture and categorize it systematically.

**Protocol for inbound WhatsApp feedback:**

1. Acknowledge within 2 hours during operating hours (7am-8pm)
2. Categorize the message into one of the 5 buckets (see Section 4)
3. Log it in the tracking sheet before responding
4. Respond with empathy + action, not just acknowledgment

### Channel 2 — Monthly Check-in (structured)

A simple 3-question WhatsApp message sent to all active subscribers on the 1st of each month.

Keep it to 3 questions maximum — more than 3 and response rates collapse.

**Monthly check-in questions:**

1. "Rate this month's juices: 🔴 Need work / 🟡 Good / 🟢 Love it"
2. "Any specific juice you want more of, or a new blend you'd like us to try?"
3. "How are you feeling vs 30 days ago?" (open text — can be one word, they decide)

Send at 10am on the 1st of the month. Follow up with non-responders once, on the 3rd. No more than two prompts per month.

### Channel 3 — Post-Trial Survey (conversion checkpoint)

A 5-question survey sent at the end of the free week trial, before the subscription conversion ask.

This is the most important feedback moment — the customer has experienced the product but not committed. Their honest assessment is most valuable here.

**Post-trial survey questions:**

1. Which juice was your favourite this week?
2. What could we improve — taste, packaging, delivery timing, or something else?
3. What price per bottle feels fair to you?
4. Would you recommend Soma to a friend? (NPS: rate 1-10)
5. What almost stopped you from trying Soma in the first place?

Send via WhatsApp as a casual message, not a Google Form link. Keep it conversational. Log all responses in the tracking sheet.

### Channel 4 — Quarterly Wellness Review (depth)

For subscribers who have been active for 90+ days. A longer conversation — done over WhatsApp voice note or a quick call if the customer is open to it.

Purpose: celebrate their progress, understand the real health impact they are experiencing, ask deep questions about what Soma means to them. This is also the richest material for brand storytelling.

**Quarterly review questions:**

1. What has changed in how you feel since you started?
2. Which products have become part of your daily routine, and which have you stopped ordering?
3. What would make Soma even more useful for your wellness goals?
4. Is there anyone in your life who you think would benefit from Soma?

### Channel 5 — Exit Survey (mandatory before churn)

For every subscriber who cancels or pauses, a brief exit conversation before processing the wallet refund.

This is mandatory. No refund processed without logging the exit reason.

**Exit survey questions:**

1. What made you decide to pause or cancel?
2. Was there a specific product, delivery, or service issue?
3. Would you come back? What would need to change?
4. Any final feedback for us?

These answers are the most valuable in the entire feedback system. Churned customers tell the truth.

## 3. Response Protocol

Speed and tone are both critical.

| Feedback type | Response time | Resolution time | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Delivery issue (missing, late, wrong item) | Within 2 hours | Within 24 hours | Apologetic, solution-first |
| Taste complaint | Within 24 hours | Logged, investigated at next batch | Genuinely curious, not defensive |
| Positive feedback | Within 24 hours | N/A | Warm, personal, ask to share |
| Price concern | Within 24 hours | Explain value, do not discount | Educational, empathetic |
| Health concern or adverse reaction | Immediately | Immediately | Take seriously, document fully |

**For positive feedback:** thank the customer, then ask: "Would you be open to sharing this with our WhatsApp community? Even one sentence helps." Do not ask them to write a Google review — that feels transactional. WhatsApp community sharing is natural and in-context.

**For health concerns or adverse reactions:** take every single one seriously regardless of how unlikely contamination seems. Collect: batch details, pressed date, what they consumed, what symptoms they experienced. Notify insurance if any hospitalisation. Document in a separate incident log, not the general feedback sheet.

## 4. Feedback Categorization

Every piece of feedback — inbound WhatsApp, survey response, or verbal during a delivery — goes into one of five buckets:

| Bucket | What it covers |
|---|---|
| Taste | Flavour, sweetness level, intensity, texture, smell |
| Delivery | Timing, temperature, missing items, packaging damage |
| Price | Too expensive, price comparison, value perception |
| Health Impact | Positive or negative effects experienced |
| Service | Communication, responsiveness, onboarding, billing |

Track volume per bucket weekly. If one bucket is receiving disproportionate feedback, it is a signal that something systemic needs attention.

## 5. Tracking System

### Phase 1 — Google Sheets (launch through ~100 subscribers)

One sheet, one row per feedback item.

**Columns:**
Date | Customer Name | Channel | Bucket | Severity (1-3) | Status (Open / In Progress / Done) | Action Taken | Notes

**Severity scale:**

- 1 — Minor (preference, suggestion, positive note)
- 2 — Moderate (recurring complaint, product issue that affected experience)
- 3 — Critical (health concern, delivery failure, customer at risk of churn)

**Status definitions:**

- Open — logged, not yet actioned
- In Progress — being investigated or resolved
- Done — resolved and communicated back to customer

Review all Open items every Monday. Nothing stays Open for more than 7 days without a status update.

### Phase 2 — Dedicated tool (when team grows or volume exceeds 30 feedback items/week)

Evaluate Freshdesk, Notion database, or Airtable. The key requirement is that feedback is linked to individual customers so patterns per customer are visible alongside product-level patterns.

## 6. Iteration Cycle

**Every 4 weeks**, review all feedback from the period. Extract the top 3 recurring issues or opportunities. These become the action items for the next cycle.

**Rules for the iteration cycle:**

- No more than 3 changes per month. More than 3 introduces chaos into production and customer communication.
- Changes to recipes go through the tasting process (see below) before rollout.
- Changes to delivery or operations can be implemented immediately if they do not affect product quality.
- Positive feedback informs what to protect, not just what to change. If a blend is consistently rated 🟢, do not mess with it.

**Monthly iteration review questions:**

1. What are the top 3 complaints this month?
2. What are the top 3 things customers love?
3. Is there a pattern in churn — do people cancel after a specific blend, or at a specific week in the subscription?
4. What feedback has been ignored for 2+ cycles and still persists? That one needs action.

## 7. Recipe Iteration Process

When taste feedback triggers a recipe change, follow this process — do not change recipes based on a single complaint.

**Trigger:** 3 or more customers report the same issue with the same blend within one month.

**Process:**

1. Log the pattern in the feedback sheet with all 3+ references linked
2. Identify the specific issue: too bitter, too sweet, too thin, too intense, wrong aftertaste
3. Develop a variation in a small test batch (5-10 bottles)
4. Select 5 existing customers who have ordered that blend — preferably those who reported the issue and those who gave it positive feedback
5. Offer them the test variation alongside the current version, ask for a direct comparison
6. If 4 of 5 prefer the variation (or the issue is resolved): implement in next regular batch
7. If results are mixed: iterate again or hold

**Communication when a change is implemented:**

Always tell customers. "You asked for a milder ginger shot. We tested three batches and found a ratio that 4 of 5 taste-testers preferred. Try the new version this week and tell us what you think."

This transparency is brand-building. It shows customers their feedback has teeth.

## 8. What Not to Change Based on Feedback

The following are non-negotiable regardless of customer feedback:

- **Short shelf life.** If a customer asks why the juice expires the same day, educate them: shelf life is the freshness indicator. No preservatives means it expires fast. That is the feature, not the bug.
- **No preservatives.** If a customer asks for a version that lasts longer in the fridge, explain why Soma does not make that version.
- **No added sweeteners.** If customers find certain blends too bitter, the answer is recipe iteration (less of the bitter ingredient) — not adding sugar, honey, or artificial sweeteners.
- **No artificial flavours or colours.** The beet blend is deep red because of beets. It is not a branding decision.

Feedback that pushes against these gets a warm, educational response — not a product change.

## 9. NPS Tracking

Calculate NPS monthly from post-trial survey responses (Question 4: rate 1-10).

**NPS calculation:**
- Promoters: scores 9-10
- Passives: scores 7-8
- Detractors: scores 0-6
- NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors

**Targets:**

| Month | NPS Target |
|---|---|
| Month 1-2 | Above 20 (baseline, sample size small) |
| Month 3-4 | Above 35 |
| Month 6 | Above 50 |
| Month 12 | Above 60 |

**If NPS drops below 30:** stop and investigate. Do not continue marketing or acquisition activity until the underlying issue is identified and addressed. A product with an NPS below 30 is not ready to scale.

**Monthly NPS log (Google Sheet):**
Month | Responses | Promoters | Passives | Detractors | NPS | Notes

## 10. Feedback-to-Product Wins Log

Maintain a running log of cases where customer feedback directly led to a product or service improvement. This serves two purposes:

1. Internal evidence that the feedback system works — motivates the team to take feedback seriously
2. Community storytelling material — share wins publicly with customers

**Format for each win:**

> **[Date] — [Blend or Service area]**
> What customers said: "[direct quote or paraphrase]"
> What we changed: [specific change]
> Result: [what customers said after the change]

Share these monthly in the WhatsApp community as a short message: "This month's Soma update — you asked, we listened." Even one win per month is enough. Customers who see their feedback acted on become the most loyal and vocal advocates.

---

## 11. The Feedback Flywheel

Better product → happier customers → more referrals → more customers → more feedback → better product.

The system only works if feedback is actioned. Collecting feedback without acting on it is worse than collecting nothing — customers who gave feedback and saw nothing change will not give feedback again, and will churn quietly.

Every feedback item logged is a commitment to respond. Every iteration completed is proof the brand is alive.
