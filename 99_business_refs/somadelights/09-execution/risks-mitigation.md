# 23 — Risks & Mitigation

> Top 20 risks for Soma Delights, structured with severity, likelihood, early warning signs, prevention, and fallback plans. Specific to a home-kitchen cold-pressed subscription business in Hyderabad.

---

## Risk Severity Scale

| Level | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Critical** | Can kill the business within weeks |
| **High** | Will seriously damage growth or economics if not addressed |
| **Medium** | Causes friction, inefficiency, or slow bleed |
| **Low** | Annoyance, manageable with basic attention |

## Risk Likelihood Scale

| Level | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Very Likely** | Will almost certainly happen |
| **Likely** | Probably happens within first 6 months |
| **Possible** | Could happen, depends on execution |
| **Unlikely** | Low probability but high impact if it does |

---

## Risk Register

### 1. Too Many SKUs at Launch

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Very Likely |
| **Why it matters** | Every additional SKU multiplies production complexity, ingredient sourcing, waste risk, and label inventory. At home-kitchen scale, 8 SKUs means 8 different ingredient sets, 8 different prep sequences, and 8 different ways to screw up quality. You're a solo founder, not a factory. |
| **Early Warning** | Prep time exceeding 2 hours. More than 2 ingredients going bad before use. Customer confusion about what to order. |
| **Prevention** | Launch with exactly 5 SKUs. No exceptions. No "just one more seasonal special." The constraint is the feature — it forces you to make 5 excellent products instead of 10 mediocre ones. |
| **Fallback** | If you've already launched too many, kill the bottom 2 performers by volume immediately. Tell customers "seasonal rotation" — they'll understand. |

### 2. Poor Subscription Retention

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Critical |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | The entire business model depends on recurring revenue. If subscribers churn after 2–3 weeks, your CAC never pays back, your route density drops, and your production planning becomes impossible. A subscription business with 50% monthly churn is just a retail business with extra steps. |
| **Early Warning** | Week 2 reorder rate below 60%. Customers skipping days without requesting pause. WhatsApp messages going unread. |
| **Prevention** | Education booklet from Day 1 — customers who understand "why" they're drinking this retain 3x longer. Weekly WhatsApp check-in: "How are you feeling this week?" Flexible pause option (don't make cancelling easier than pausing). Rotate flavors within subscription to prevent boredom. |
| **Fallback** | If retention is below 50% at Month 2, the problem is product-market fit, not marketing. Survey churned customers. Are they leaving because of taste, price, inconvenience, or lack of results? Fix the root cause before spending more on acquisition. |

### 3. Delivery Inconsistency

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Critical |
| **Likelihood** | Very Likely |
| **Why it matters** | The milkman model lives and dies on consistency. One late delivery is forgivable. Three late deliveries and the habit breaks — the customer stops expecting it, stops consuming it, and cancels. Delivery consistency IS the product. |
| **Early Warning** | On-time delivery dropping below 95%. Customer messages saying "haven't received today's bottle." Rider calling in sick with no backup. |
| **Prevention** | Fixed delivery window (6–8 AM), same route order daily. Rider backup plan: founder is always the backup rider. Route designed with 15-min buffer. GPS tracking on rider phone. Pre-dawn production completion target: 5:30 AM. |
| **Fallback** | If rider no-shows: founder delivers. If traffic/weather disrupts: pre-notify customers via WhatsApp broadcast. If chronic late delivery to specific apartments: move them to beginning of route or evaluate if they're too far from cluster. |

### 4. Weak Quality Control / Bad Batch

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Critical |
| **Likelihood** | Possible |
| **Why it matters** | One bad bottle — off taste, visible sediment, stomach upset — and that customer tells 10 people. In a word-of-mouth business, a quality failure is a marketing failure multiplied. If someone gets food poisoning, it's a legal and reputational catastrophe. |
| **Early Warning** | Inconsistent taste between batches. Unusual color or separation. Any customer complaint about taste or stomach issues. Ingredient quality varying between suppliers. |
| **Prevention** | Taste-test every batch before dispatch (non-negotiable). Consistent recipes with measured quantities (not "some ginger"). Temperature logging from production to delivery. Reject any ingredient that doesn't look/smell right. Batch numbering for traceability. |
| **Fallback** | If a bad batch goes out: immediate WhatsApp message to affected customers, full refund + free replacement, honest explanation. Log the incident, trace the cause, implement corrective action. One honest response to a mistake builds more trust than pretending it didn't happen. |

### 5. Spoilage and Wastage Exceeding 10%

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | Cold-pressed juice has a 24-hour shelf life. If you produce 30 bottles and only deliver 25, those 5 bottles are pure loss — ingredients, labor, packaging, all wasted. At 55% gross margin, 10% wastage eats 5.5% of margin. At 20% wastage, your business is bleeding. |
| **Early Warning** | More than 2 bottles going undelivered per day. Ingredients expiring before use. Overproduction on weekends when some subscribers pause. |
| **Prevention** | Production planning based on confirmed orders only (not forecasts). Evening WhatsApp confirmation: "Tomorrow's delivery confirmed?" for new subscribers. Start with slight underproduction — it's better to sell out than throw away. Use pulp/residue for experimental products. |
| **Fallback** | Excess bottles → sell at discounted rate to apartment security/staff (builds goodwill). Overripe fruits → make pulp products or freeze for smoothie packs. Track wastage daily — if it's trending up, reduce production quantities. |

### 6. Wrong Pricing (Too High or Too Low)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Possible |
| **Why it matters** | Too high: trial conversion drops, customers compare to Rs 30 Tropicana packet. Too low: margins don't support the delivery model, you can't hire help, business slowly dies. In Hyderabad's KPHB/Miyapur demographic, the sweet spot is narrow: Rs 99–149 per bottle. |
| **Early Warning** | Trial → subscriber conversion below 30% (too high). Negative unit economics even at 50 orders/day (too low). Customers consistently asking for discounts (perceived value gap). |
| **Prevention** | Price based on unit economics, not competition. Calculate: COGS + packaging + delivery cost per unit + 15% overhead → minimum viable price. Then add your margin. Test with 10 customers at your target price before committing. Frame as daily cost (Rs 120/day), not monthly cost (Rs 3,600/month). |
| **Fallback** | If too high: introduce a smaller size (200ml at Rs 79) rather than discounting the main product. If too low: raise prices for new subscribers, grandfather existing subscribers for 1 month, then normalize. Never discount — add value instead (extra wellness card, free sample of new SKU). |

### 7. Low Density (Customers Spread Too Thin)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | If your 50 customers are in 50 different apartment complexes across 20 km, delivery cost per customer makes the model unviable. The math only works with density: 8–15 customers per complex, 3–5 complexes per route. |
| **Early Warning** | Delivery time exceeding 2 hours for 30 orders. Fuel cost per delivery above Rs 10. Taking orders from areas outside your core zone "just to grow numbers." |
| **Prevention** | Hard geographic boundary from Day 1: only accept orders from KPHB Colony, Miyapur, and Chandanagar. Refuse orders from Madhapur, Gachibowli, or other "nearby" areas — they're not nearby at 6 AM. Concentrate pop-ups in 3–4 specific apartment complexes. |
| **Fallback** | If you've already spread thin: identify your 3 densest clusters, double down on growth there, and politely sunset one-off distant customers with "we're focusing our delivery on [your area] — we'll let you know when we expand to yours." |

### 8. Poor Subscription Design (Too Rigid or Too Complex)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Medium |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | Too rigid: "You must take the same juice every day for 30 days" — customers feel trapped, churn increases. Too complex: "Choose from 8 SKUs, pick 3 per day, rotate weekly, swap monthly" — customers get decision fatigue, never commit. |
| **Early Warning** | Customers asking to customize every single order. High cancellation rate in first week. Customers saying "I'll just order when I want" instead of subscribing. |
| **Prevention** | Two plans only at launch. Morning Routine (daily green juice) and Hydration Habit (daily fruit-forward juice). Within each plan, you rotate the specific SKU — customer trusts your selection. Add customization only after 100 subscribers when you have the operational bandwidth. |
| **Fallback** | If plans aren't working: offer a "Taster Week" (7 different bottles, 7 days, Rs 699) as an on-ramp to subscription. Low commitment, high variety, and you learn their preferences. Then upsell to monthly subscription based on what they liked. |

### 9. Customer Education Burden

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Medium |
| **Likelihood** | Very Likely |
| **Why it matters** | "Micro-wellness" is not a category people search for. Nobody wakes up thinking "I need a micro-wellness subscription." You have to create the understanding before you create the demand. But if education takes too much effort per customer, acquisition cost becomes prohibitive. |
| **Early Warning** | First conversation with potential customer takes 15+ minutes. Customers saying "so it's just juice?" after your pitch. Low booklet engagement (people not scanning QR codes). |
| **Prevention** | The 3-layer education system: (1) WhatsApp message with the delivery — 2 lines on today's ingredient benefit, (2) QR code on bottle linking to a 60-second video, (3) Monthly printed booklet with deeper wellness content. Don't try to educate before purchase — educate during consumption. The product sells on trust and taste; the education builds retention. |
| **Fallback** | If education materials aren't engaging: simplify. One bold claim per bottle: "This bottle has more Vitamin C than 3 oranges." People remember one fact, not a paragraph. Use customer testimonials — "Ravi in B-Block says his energy improved in 2 weeks" is more convincing than any nutrition science. |

### 10. Operational Overwhelm (Founder Burnout)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Critical |
| **Likelihood** | Very Likely |
| **Why it matters** | 4 AM wake-ups, 7 days a week, doing production + delivery + customer service + growth + admin = burnout within 8–12 weeks. And when the founder burns out, the business stops — because the founder IS the business at this stage. |
| **Early Warning** | Dreading the alarm clock. Cutting corners on quality. Snapping at customers. Skipping pop-ups because "too tired." Getting sick more than once a month. |
| **Prevention** | Non-negotiable: 1 day off per week from Week 4. Sunday = no delivery (subscribers know this from Day 1). 9 PM sleep, no exceptions. Hire kitchen helper by customer 20, not customer 50. Accept that 80% execution sustained > 100% execution that crashes. |
| **Fallback** | If burnout hits: take 3 days off, have a friend/family member handle deliveries (brief them on route). Evaluate whether you're doing tasks that should be delegated. Emergency hire for kitchen help — better to overspend on labor for a month than lose the business. |

### 11. Compliance Mistakes (FSSAI, Labeling)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Possible |
| **Why it matters** | FSSAI violation fine: up to Rs 5 lakh. Selling without registration: criminal offense. Incorrect labeling: product seizure. A single regulatory action, even if resolved, creates local news and kills word-of-mouth trust. |
| **Early Warning** | Operating without FSSAI registration receipt. Labels missing mandatory elements. No batch numbers on bottles. No temperature logs. |
| **Prevention** | Apply for FSSAI Basic Registration before your first sale (1 hour, Rs 100). Use the labeling checklist in doc 21. Maintain daily production and temperature logs. Keep all receipts and certifications in one folder. |
| **Fallback** | If an inspector visits and finds issues: cooperate fully, fix immediately, document corrections. Most inspectors for basic registration are reasonable if you show good faith. If a serious violation: pause operations, fix everything, then resume. Better to lose a week than lose the license. |

### 12. Bad Route Design (Inefficient Delivery)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Medium |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | An inefficient route means: more fuel cost, more time, more late deliveries, earlier wake-up time, faster rider burnout. At 100 customers, the difference between a good route and a bad route is 45 minutes and Rs 100/day in fuel. Over a month, that's Rs 3,000 and 22.5 hours. |
| **Early Warning** | Delivery round exceeding 2.5 hours. Rider zigzagging between areas. Same-complex customers getting deliveries 30 minutes apart. |
| **Prevention** | Map all customers on Google Maps. Group by apartment complex, then sequence by geographic proximity. Start from production kitchen, nearest cluster first, furthest last. Optimize weekly as new customers are added. Always add new customers to existing clusters, not new areas. |
| **Fallback** | If routes are already messy: reset. Take a Sunday, map everything, redesign from scratch. Communicate new delivery windows to affected customers. The short-term disruption of a route change is better than the long-term cost of an inefficient route. |

### 13. Referral Fraud (Self-Referral, Gaming)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Low |
| **Likelihood** | Possible |
| **Why it matters** | Your referral system (10% for 12 months, cashable wallet after Rs 500) is generous. Some people will try to game it — referring themselves with a different phone number, creating fake accounts, or colluding with friends for mutual referral. At small scale, each fraudulent referral costs Rs 50–150/month. |
| **Early Warning** | Same delivery address with multiple "different" accounts. Referrer and referee with the same last name and adjacent flat numbers. Bursts of referrals that don't convert to sustained subscriptions. |
| **Prevention** | Manual review of all referrals (easy at < 200 customers). One referral per household. Wallet cashout only after referee completes 4 weeks of active subscription (this is the best fraud prevention — gaming requires real money spent). Referral credit is 10% of referee's actual purchases, not sign-up. |
| **Fallback** | If fraud detected: quietly stop crediting that referrer's wallet. Don't accuse — just apply the "referral under review" status. At this scale, fraud will be 1–2 cases, not systemic. Don't build complex anti-fraud systems for a problem that affects < 1% of customers. |

### 14. Ingredient Cost Volatility (Seasonal Price Swings)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Medium |
| **Likelihood** | Very Likely |
| **Why it matters** | Vegetable and fruit prices in Hyderabad can swing 50–200% seasonally. Spinach at Rs 30/kg in winter can hit Rs 80/kg in summer. Green apple (imported) fluctuates with exchange rates and supply. If your COGS jumps 40% and your price stays fixed, margins collapse. |
| **Early Warning** | Weekly ingredient cost tracking shows 20%+ increase on any key ingredient. Rythu Bazaar prices trending up for 2+ weeks. Supplier warning about upcoming shortage. |
| **Prevention** | Design recipes with substitution flexibility: spinach ↔ kale ↔ palak. Build seasonal menu rotations (summer vs. winter SKUs). Maintain 2–3 suppliers per ingredient — never single-source. Set maximum COGS per SKU and reformulate if exceeded. Buffer your pricing with 10% ingredient cost headroom. |
| **Fallback** | If a key ingredient spikes: swap to seasonal alternative, communicate to customers as "seasonal special rotation." If across-the-board increase: absorb for 2 weeks while watching trend, then adjust pricing for new subscribers while keeping existing subscriber prices stable for 1 month. |

### 15. Kitchen Capacity Bottleneck

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | A home kitchen with one cold-press juicer has a ceiling: roughly 80–100 bottles per 4-hour production window. That's 80–100 customers maximum. Beyond that, you either need a second juicer, a larger space, or both. Hitting this ceiling without planning means turning away customers — the worst possible growth killer. |
| **Early Warning** | Production window expanding beyond 4 hours. Quality dropping on later batches (rushing). Kitchen feeling cramped with 2 people. Fridge can't hold all finished products. |
| **Prevention** | Know your ceiling: time how many bottles your juicer produces per hour, multiply by your production window. Plan the transition to a larger space at 70% of capacity, not 100%. Start looking for commercial kitchen space at 60 customers. Second juicer purchase at 80 customers. |
| **Fallback** | If you hit capacity suddenly: pause new subscriber intake, focus on retention and quality for existing customers. "Waitlist" for new subscribers — this actually creates scarcity and demand. Meanwhile, fast-track the kitchen expansion. |

### 16. Cold Chain Failure (Juice Delivered Warm)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | Cold-pressed juice at room temperature for 2+ hours in Hyderabad's 35–42 deg C summer = bacterial growth, taste degradation, safety risk. A customer receiving a warm bottle will not drink it, will not reorder, and will tell their neighbors. |
| **Early Warning** | Customer feedback about "room temperature" delivery. Delivery round exceeding 2 hours in summer. Ice packs in bag fully melted before last delivery. Rider not using insulated bag properly. |
| **Prevention** | Insulated delivery bags (Rs 1,500 each, non-negotiable). Gel ice packs (reusable, Rs 50–100 each, 4 per bag). Production completes by 5:30 AM so bottles are fully chilled before loading. Route designed so furthest customer is under 2 hours from kitchen. Spot-check bottle temperature at delivery with probe thermometer. |
| **Fallback** | If cold chain fails on a delivery: replace the bottle next day for free, apologize personally. If systemic (summer heat too much): add more ice packs, split route to reduce time, consider insulated box instead of bag, or move delivery earlier (5:30 AM start). |

### 17. Copycat Competition in Same Neighborhoods

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Medium |
| **Likelihood** | Possible |
| **Why it matters** | If Soma Delights succeeds in KPHB/Miyapur, someone will copy it — probably within 6–9 months. Same juices, same delivery model, maybe cheaper. In Hyderabad, food business copycats are fast and shameless. |
| **Early Warning** | New juice delivery flyers in your apartment complexes. Customers mentioning "another juice delivery service." Similar-looking products appearing at local gyms. |
| **Prevention** | Your moat is not the juice — it's the system. Education booklets, habit architecture, wellness tracking, community events, referral loyalty. A copycat can duplicate your menu but not your customer relationships. Build the relationship depth so switching costs are emotional, not just financial. Density advantage: once you have 15 customers in a complex, a new entrant can't compete on delivery cost. |
| **Fallback** | If a copycat appears: do NOT compete on price. Instead, double down on education, community, and product innovation. Launch your next product phase (pulp, fermented shots) to stay ahead. The copycat will likely under-invest in quality and burn out — most food copycats in Hyderabad last 3–4 months before quitting. |

### 18. Seasonal Demand Dip (Winter = Less Juice Demand?)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Medium |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | Hyderabad winter (November–January) is mild by Indian standards (15–22 deg C mornings) but people drink less cold beverages. New subscriber sign-ups may slow. Existing subscribers may pause. If you've staffed up for summer demand, winter dip hits payroll. |
| **Early Warning** | October sign-up rate declining. Existing subscribers asking to pause. Lower trial conversion at pop-ups. |
| **Prevention** | Introduce warm/room-temperature options for winter: ginger-turmeric shots (served at room temp), warm spiced apple cider, warm beetroot latte. These use the same ingredients and kitchen setup. Frame as "winter wellness" rotation. Also: winter is when fermented shots (room temp, gut health) make perfect sense — time the launch for October. |
| **Fallback** | If winter demand still drops: reduce production proportionally, shift kitchen helper to fewer days, use the slower period for product development and system improvement. Don't panic-discount to maintain volume — it trains customers to expect deals. |

### 19. Cash Flow Crunch (Month 2–3)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | High |
| **Likelihood** | Likely |
| **Why it matters** | Month 1 you invest in equipment + ingredients. Month 2 you hire help. Revenue is growing but expenses are front-loaded. If you started with Rs 2L budget and spent Rs 1.5L on setup, you have Rs 50,000 for 2 months of operations. That's tight. One bad month and you can't buy ingredients. |
| **Early Warning** | Bank balance below Rs 30,000. Delaying supplier payments. Considering skipping rider fuel allowance. Thinking about taking personal debt for business expenses. |
| **Prevention** | Keep Rs 50,000 as untouchable emergency reserve. Weekly cash flow tracking (not monthly — you need to see problems coming). Collect subscription payments upfront (weekly or monthly, not per-delivery). Negotiate 7-day payment terms with ingredient suppliers. Minimize fixed costs: part-time hires, not full-time, until revenue justifies it. |
| **Fallback** | If cash runs low: cut non-essential spending first (label upgrades, website, new equipment). Shift to daily purchasing of ingredients (no waste). Ask suppliers for 15-day credit. If truly critical: pause new customer intake, serve existing customers profitably, rebuild cash reserves. Do NOT borrow at high interest to "grow faster." |

### 20. Single Point of Failure (Founder Is Everything)

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Severity** | Critical |
| **Likelihood** | Very Likely |
| **Why it matters** | If you get sick for 3 days, the business stops. If you travel for a family emergency, customers don't get delivered. If you burn out, everything collapses. This is the most fundamental risk of a solo-founder food business — and the hardest to solve because solving it requires money you don't have yet. |
| **Early Warning** | You haven't taken a single day off in 6+ weeks. Nobody else knows your recipes. Nobody else knows the delivery route. Nobody has access to the WhatsApp business account. Customer relationships are all in your head. |
| **Prevention** | Document everything from Day 1 — recipes with exact measurements, route maps, customer lists with preferences, supplier contacts, production SOPs. By Month 2, your kitchen helper should be able to produce without you (even if quality is 80% of your standard). Identify one person (friend, family, neighbor) who can do emergency delivery if you're down. WhatsApp Business account with saved replies. |
| **Fallback** | If founder is incapacitated: activate emergency protocol — kitchen helper produces standard SKUs only, emergency delivery person follows route sheet, WhatsApp auto-reply says "Soma Delights is operating with a temporary schedule, thank you for your patience." Survivable for 3–5 days with preparation. Beyond that, you need Stage 1 hiring to be complete. |

## Top 10 Risks to Actively Monitor (Ranked)

1. **Founder burnout** — the business cannot survive without you yet. Protect yourself first.
2. **Delivery inconsistency** — consistency IS the product. If delivery breaks, the habit breaks.
3. **Poor retention** — if week 2 reorder rate is below 60%, stop growing and fix the product.
4. **Single point of failure** — document everything, train someone, have a backup plan.
5. **Bad batch / quality failure** — one sick customer can end the business. No shortcuts on food safety.
6. **Low density** — refuse customers outside your zone. Density is more important than total numbers.
7. **Cold chain failure** — warm juice in Hyderabad summer is a health risk, not just a quality issue.
8. **Wastage > 10%** — produce to order, not to forecast. Every wasted bottle is wasted margin.
9. **Cash flow crunch** — track weekly, maintain reserves, collect upfront.
10. **Too many SKUs** — 5 at launch, no exceptions. Complexity is the enemy of a solo founder.

---

*Every one of these risks is manageable — if you see it coming. The companies that die from these risks are the ones that were "too busy" to monitor them. Build the habit of reviewing this list every Sunday evening. 15 minutes of risk awareness saves months of crisis management.*
